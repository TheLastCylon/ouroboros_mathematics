# The Sphere
### Dirk J. Botha, April 2026

---

## 1. Where the Boundary Goes

In [`the_boundary.md`](the_boundary.md) we established that the four
$S^+$ transitions are not failures -- they are junction points where the
geometry continues through a denominator of zero.

The question left open was: continues where?

The unit circle lives in a plane. At the four boundary points, the
$\mathbin{:}$ relationship is still real, still carries a value, still
has direction. If it cannot continue within the plane, there is only one
place it can go:

**Perpendicular to it.**

A third coordinate. Call it $z$.

---

## 2. The Two Planes

The four boundary points organise themselves naturally:

- $\cos(\theta) = 0$ at $90°$ and $270°$ -- $\tan$ and $\sec$ both
  transition here. These two points, together with the $z$-axis, define
  a plane: the $yz$-plane.

- $\sin(\theta) = 0$ at $0°$ and $180°$ -- $\csc$ and $\cot$ both
  transition here. These two points, together with the $z$-axis, define
  a plane: the $xz$-plane.

Two planes. Perpendicular to each other. Each containing two of the
four boundary points. This is not an arrangement we chose -- it is what
the geometry produces when the boundary is followed honestly.

---

## 3. The Sphere

The unit circle, together with its $z$-extensions at the four boundary
points, is no longer a flat object. The minimal surface that contains
the unit circle and closes all four $z$-extensions into a single
consistent object is:

**A sphere.**

Not constructed. Not assumed. Produced -- by following the $\mathbin{:}$
geometry to where it leads.

- The unit circle is the equator.
- The $z$-axis is the axis.
- The four boundary points sit on the equator where the two perpendicular
  planes cross it.
- At the top of the $z$-axis, all four extensions arrive simultaneously
  at a single point: **Oro** -- the universal closure state, the one
  point at which every $S^+$ transition arrives.

This sphere -- the unit circle as equator, the $z$-axis through Oro --
is the sphere described by Bernhard Riemann in the 19th century, known
as the **Riemann sphere**. Riemann constructed it algebraically, via the
complex number system. Here it has not been constructed at all. It has
been produced -- by following the $\mathbin{:}$ geometry honestly, using
nothing beyond three real coordinates and the ratio operator.

The Riemann sphere was always the natural home of trigonometry. The
geometry knew. The algebra took a longer route to get there.

## 4. Complex Numbers as Consequence

For centuries, mathematicians encountered equations whose solutions had
no place on the real number line. The square root of a negative number
had no home in the algebra of the time. The response was to invent one
-- a new kind of number, defined by the rule $i^2 = -1$, called
**imaginary**.

The name is the confession. They called it imaginary because they could
not see what it was pointing at.

The $z$-axis -- the third coordinate forced by the geometry at the $S^+$
boundary -- is precisely what the imaginary unit was invented to describe.
Not a new kind of number. Not an algebraic invention. A real geometric
direction that 2D algebraic notation had no symbol for.

$$i \text{ is not } \sqrt{-1}. \text{ It is the } z\text{-axis.}$$

Every point on the Riemann sphere has three real coordinates $(x, y, z)$.
What algebra calls a complex number $a + bi$ is the stereographic
projection of a sphere point onto a flat plane. The $b$ component --
which algebra calls imaginary -- corresponds to the $z$-coordinate of
the original sphere point.

The mapping is accurate. But the flat plane and its notation obscure
what is actually a real coordinate on a real geometric object.

### Oro and the north pole

Algebra calls the north pole of the Riemann sphere undefined, or
infinity, because stereographic projection maps it onto a flat plane
that has no room for it -- the projection line runs parallel to the plane
and never lands.

Stand on the north pole of any sphere and observe: every direction you
face is south. There is no north from the north pole. Every path, from
every direction, leads to the same point when it arrives here.

This is the geometric meaning of Oro. Every $S^+$ transition -- $\tan$
from $90°$, $\sec$ from $270°$, $\csc$ from $0°$, $\cot$ from $180°$
-- approaches from a different direction and arrives at the same point.
All transitions are Oro-equivalent.

Algebra saw a point that appeared to be in every direction
simultaneously and called it infinity. The sphere calls it north. It is
a real point at $(0, 0, 1)$. Nothing is undefined. Nothing is infinite.
The flat plane simply had no room for it.

## 5. The Number System

Remove the complex number system -- an algebra artifact, as shown above
-- and ask what remains.

The real number line contains two kinds of numbers:

**Rational numbers** -- numbers expressible as a $\mathbin{:}$ ratio of
two integers: $1 \mathbin{:} 2$, $3 \mathbin{:} 4$, $7 \mathbin{:} 1$.
These are not merely compatible with the $\mathbin{:}$ operator. They
*are* the $\mathbin{:}$ operator applied to integers.

**Irrational numbers** -- numbers that cannot be expressed as a ratio
of integers. These are geometric quantities:

$$\begin{aligned}
\pi    &= C \mathbin{:} d   && \text{circumference to diameter} \\
\varphi &= (a+b) \mathbin{:} a && \text{the golden ratio} \\
\sqrt{2} &= \text{hyp} \mathbin{:} \text{side} && \text{diagonal of a unit square} \\
\end{aligned}$$

Not algebraic inventions. Geometric facts. Each one is a $\mathbin{:}$
relationship that cannot be reduced to a ratio of integers -- not because
it is a different kind of number, but because the geometry it describes
is incommensurable with the integer grid.

Together, rational and irrational numbers form the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$.
Three real coordinates describe every point on the Riemann sphere.
Nothing beyond $\mathbb{R}$ is required.

The complex number system added imaginary numbers to handle a geometric
direction that 2D algebra could not express. In the three-coordinate
geometric framework, that direction is the $z$-axis. It is real. It was
always real.

**The number system is $\mathbb{R}$. It was always $\mathbb{R}$.**
